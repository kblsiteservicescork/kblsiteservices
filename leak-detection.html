Leak detection & draught tracking

Knowing the number isn't enough. You need to know where.

An air tightness test tells you how much air is escaping. Leak detection tells you exactly where it's coming from — so it can be fixed, not estimated.

How it works

While the blower door holds the building under pressure, we move through the building with a thermal imaging camera and smoke pencils. Under pressure, every gap that passes air becomes visible — cold air shows as a temperature differential on the thermal camera, and smoke traces the exact path of movement at joints, penetrations and junctions.

Every leak found is photographed, logged and mapped to the relevant part of the building fabric.

On new builds and retrofits

When a building fails its air tightness test, the number alone doesn't tell you what to fix. Leak detection turns a failed result into a remediation list. We locate the specific penetrations, junctions or unsealed details that are causing the failure so your team can address them before re-test.

On retrofits, leak detection is used before works begin to map existing failure points, and again after to confirm they've been resolved.

  • Structural penetrations — pipes, cables, beams
  • Junctions between wall, floor and ceiling elements
  • Service voids and boxing
  • Attic hatches and roof interfaces
  • Window and door reveals

On existing homes

For homeowners, leak detection is what turns a vague feeling — "the house is cold" — into a specific, fixable list. We locate where cold air is entering the living space, rank the gaps by the volume of air they're passing, and give you a report that tells a tradesperson or a DIY-capable homeowner exactly what to seal.

The gaps that matter most are rarely the obvious ones. Doors and windows account for a fraction of air loss in most buildings. The real culprits are structural penetrations, service voids and junctions that were never detailed for airtightness — because nobody could see them failing.
